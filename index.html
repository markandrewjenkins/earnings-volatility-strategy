The edge — selling earnings volatility

Quarterly earnings often account for the majority of a stock's annual volatility. Ahead of the event, hedgers (price-insensitive, mandated to buy protection) and speculators (buying short-dated calls) push option prices and implied volatility systematically above what the stock actually delivers. This strategy takes the other side: sell the inflated front-month IV just before the announcement, collect the IV crush after it.

Backed by research over 72,500 earnings events across 4,500 stocks (2007–present): traded blindly, both structures break even — the market prices earnings well on average. But three pre-event filters isolate a persistently profitable subset (~10–12% of all events):

1 · Term-structure slope

≤ −0.00406

Slope of ATM IV from the front expiry to the 45-day point. Steep backwardation = near-term options most overpriced relative to later ones. The single strongest predictor — it gates the whole trade.

2 · IV30 / RV30

≥ 1.25

30-day implied vol vs Yang-Zhang realized vol. If IV is already rich versus how the stock actually moves, it's even more likely to be overpriced for the event.

3 · Liquidity

≥ 1.5M shares

30-day average share volume. More participants → more price-insensitive hedging flow → richer premium. Higher-volume deciles showed better returns for both structures.

Ratings — the action scale

One scale, describing how strongly a name's signal reads against the strategy's criteria. These are observations about the data, not trade advice:
STRONG ★  every edge and execution filter clears · MODERATE  the research's three edge filters clear, but an execution check does not · WEAK  slope clears plus exactly one other filter · NONE  the filters do not clear (muted grey deliberately — absence of signal isn't danger; coral is reserved for live warnings like FOMC ⚡). Mapping to the original research's terms: STRONG/MODERATE = "Recommended", WEAK = "Consider", NONE = "Avoid".

STRONG is a strict subset of MODERATE and is treated distinctly everywhere: its own odds base (70% vs 66%), its own sizing anchor, and precedence when the concurrency cap binds. A signal reads STRONG only when all six execution & event checks also clear: ATM spread ≤ 10% of mid, price ≥ $20, expected move ≥ 1.15× the stock's own historical earnings moves, ATM open interest ≥ 500, the earnings date confirmed (Nasdaq and Yahoo agree within a day), and no FOMC decision within ±1 trading day of the trade window. In short: MODERATE says the edge is present; STRONG says it also survives real-world execution costs. Every threshold is shown live in the Scanner tab's signal monitor.

Execution timing
  • Entry: final ~15 minutes before the close of the session preceding the announcement (today's close for AMC reporters; also covers next-morning BMO reporters).
  • Exit: ~15 minutes after the next session's open — the "jump" exit. Holding until the next close was a consistent loser in the data (post-earnings announcement drift works against a short-vol position).
  • Strike: at-the-money at entry. Calendar gap: ~30 days — wide enough that back-month IV holds while front-month IV crushes.
Risks — and how they're managed

The calendar is defined-risk on paper — the most the spread itself can lose is the debit paid — but "defined risk" describes the option structure, not everything that can happen to the position. Three real risks sit outside that debit.

Early assignment on the short leg

The front-month option that is sold is an American-style equity option: its holder may exercise at any time. If the short leg is assigned, the calendar stops being a tidy spread — the position becomes long or short 100 shares per contract at the strike, still holding the long back-month option as a hedge. Two things bite. First, assignment demands the full share notional (a $120 stock is $12,000 of stock per contract, versus a debit of a few hundred dollars), which can trigger a margin call. Second, you carry unhedged gap risk on the residual shares until they are unwound.

Why it is usually small here — and when it is not. The trade is entered at the money and held for a single overnight. An ATM option is almost never exercised early, because the holder would forfeit its remaining extrinsic (time) value; early exercise is only rational when an option is deep in-the-money with little time value left. The real trigger to watch is a dividend: a short call that is in-the-money going into an ex-dividend date is a prime early-exercise candidate, because the holder exercises to capture the dividend. A large overnight move that pushes the short leg deep ITM also raises the odds.

Practical mitigations:

  • Avoid ex-dividend dates inside the one-night hold — this removes the single biggest early-call-assignment trigger.
  • Stay at-the-money (the strategy already does): maximum extrinsic value makes early exercise irrational for the holder.
  • Prefer puts over calls when a dividend is near — short puts are not exercised early to capture dividends.
  • Hold one night and unwind at the open — if assigned, close the resulting shares and the long option immediately rather than carrying them.
  • Size for the share notional, not the debit (below).

Is the only truly safe approach to size so small you could cover an assignment yourself? In substance, yes — that is the honest answer. Debit-based sizing (a few hundred dollars per contract) understates the capital a worst-case assignment demands, which is the full share notional: contracts × 100 × strike. The robust rule of thumb is to keep that share notional across all open short legs comfortably within what the account could actually absorb — i.e. you could take delivery of (or be short) the shares and liquidate them yourself at the next open without a forced margin sale. That is a materially smaller position than the debit alone implies. It does not make the strategy risk-free, but it converts a potential margin-call blow-up into an orderly, self-managed unwind. Combined with avoiding ex-dividend dates and staying ATM for one night, assignment moves from a tail-risk landmine to a rare, coverable inconvenience.

Vega — a volatility spike

The calendar is net-long vega on the back month. In a broad volatility spike (a VIX event overlapping the trade) the back-month IV can crush alongside the front, hurting the position even if the underlying stock behaves. This is why the scanner gates out FOMC windows and flags stressed VIX regimes.

Gap & execution risk

A very large earnings move can push the stock clean past the profit zone — the calendar wants a small move. And the round-trip bid/ask, the combo spread, is a certain cost paid on every trade; on illiquid names it quietly exceeds the edge. Both are handled by the liquidity gates and the combo-spread filter shown on the Scanner tab.

Improvements & refinements

Goal: raise win rate and per-trade return over the baseline three-filter model. The first three ideas are implemented live in this scanner as the STRONG ★ signal; the rest are candidates for the backtest queue.
Implemented in the scanner (Strong signal)

A · Premium richness vs history

Compare today's option-implied expected move against the stock's own average realized earnings move (last ~8 quarters). Require expected ≥ 1.15× historical. The baseline filters only prove IV is rich vs recent calm trading; this proves it's rich vs what earnings actually do to this stock — directly targeting per-trade return.

B · Execution-quality gate

Require ATM bid/ask spread ≤ 10% of mid. The research's biggest silent killer is slippage: a 7% mean edge disappears if you give up 4% crossing wide spreads twice. Directly raises realized (not theoretical) win rate.

C · Minimum share price $20

Cheap stocks have structurally wide relative spreads and coarse strike spacing, so the "ATM" calendar is often meaningfully off-strike. Filtering them removes a cluster of poor fills and off-model losses.

C2 · ATM open interest ≥ 500

Open interest on the thinner side of the ATM pair must clear 500 contracts. Thin OI means you're negotiating with one market maker at entry and exit — the realized spread is far worse than the quoted one. (Live example from the first scan: SMPL rated RECOMMENDED with 66 contracts of OI and a 41% quoted spread — exactly the trade this filter exists to block.)

C3 · Confirmed earnings date

Nasdaq and Yahoo must agree on the date within ±1 day. A projected (unconfirmed) date that shifts means you sold a calendar around an event that isn't there — pure decay loss with no IV crush. Holds the signal below Strong until the sources agree.

Backtest queue · candidate refinements

D0 · Sector-cohort heat filter (NOW LIVE)

The study's strongest effect: quiet same-sector cohorts → median z 0.79 / 17% blowthrough vs 52% baseline; hot cohorts → the reverse. The scanner now keeps a rolling reaction_log.json of recent reporters' normalized reactions by sector and computes each candidate's cohort heat (shown in the chart's mini monitor); quiet cohorts add +0.30 log-odds, hot ones −0.15. The log fills as earnings season progresses.

D · Rank, don't just gate (NOW LIVE)

Every analyzed event gets a transparent 0–100 rank score (how far each signal clears its threshold) and a heuristic odds-of-profit (research base 66% shifted by documented nudges from the decile and conditioner studies, clamped 40–80%). Suggested size scales with the odds; the paper tracker fills best-ranked-first when the 3-position cap binds.

E · Skip binary-catalyst names (NOW LIVE as down-weight)

Biotech/specialty-pharma industries carry FDA-style tail risk unrelated to the earnings print. The scanner flags them (binary_risk) and cuts their odds by −0.20 log-odds rather than hard-excluding — the study's Health-sector result wasn't significant enough to justify a full ban.

F · Earnings-day VIX regime filter (revised by the study)

The conditioner study refutes half of this idea: relative to their own recent vol, stocks react less in stressed regimes (median z 1.67 vs 2.66 in calm ones) — and calm regimes are where the IV/RV filter is most flattered by a low denominator. What survives is the vega argument: in a VIX spike the calendar's back-month IV can crush with the front. Verdict: stay advisory, prefer the straddle structure over calendars in stressed regimes, and demand extra premium richness when VIX < 15.

G · Double calendar variant

For Strong setups with expected move ≫ strike spacing, a double calendar (one at +EM strike, one at −EM) widens the profit zone and cuts gamma pain when the stock moves ~1× the expected move — trading a bit of peak return for win rate.

H · Scale by Kelly, cap correlation

Max 3 concurrent positions and ≤ ~18% of account in simultaneous earnings risk (3 × 6%). Multiple same-day trades are nearly independent bets — the research's variance numbers assume one at a time.

I · Exit discipline on the jump

Take the exit 15 min after the open mechanically. The data says the edge is fully realized in the jump; discretionary "let it ride" holding re-exposes you to post-earnings drift, which is a negative-edge period for short vol.

Is boosting size in a drawdown strategically sound? The numbers above say boost-in-DD earns slightly more and recovers faster — but read why before copying it. Trades here are independent: after a losing streak the next trade's odds are unchanged ("the situation reverses itself soon" is the gambler's fallacy — there is no mean-reversion in your own P&L). Boost-in-DD wins only because it raises average exposure while the edge is positive; you'd get the same growth more safely by just sizing up constantly. Its true cost shows up exactly when it matters: if a drawdown is happening because the edge itself degraded (the usual reason live), boost-in-DD adds size to a broken strategy — the classic blow-up pattern. Cut-in-DD is the defensible throttle if you fear edge decay: shallower holes, but slower recoveries (longest DD stretches from 5.7 to 6.2 months) and less growth.

The sound version of the instinct: scale size on information, not on your own P&L — that's what the two-tier Moderate/Strong split already does, and what the conditioner study supports extending: downsize or skip FOMC-window trades (done — caps the signal below Strong), size down after hot sector cohorts, up after quiet ones, and demand extra richness when VIX < 15. Your own losing streak carries no information; market conditions do.

About this dashboard

A live, public research dashboard for a short-earnings-volatility strategy: ahead of a company's quarterly earnings, options systematically overprice the expected move. This strategy sells that richness with ATM calendar spreads (sell the front-month option, buy a longer-dated one at the same strike) and harvests the implied-volatility crush that follows the announcement.

The idea in one line. Earnings uncertainty inflates near-term implied volatility far more than the longer-dated month. Selling the expensive front month against the cheaper back month captures that gap when it collapses the morning after the report.

How to read the scanner. Tickers on the Trade Calendar are placed on their trade-entry day, not their earnings day. For any name whose signal reads Strong or Moderate, the calendar spread is entered around 3:45 PM ET that day and closed around 9:45 AM ET the next session, capturing the crush. Hover any ticker for its full read-out; the signal filters on the left show exactly how it scores against every criterion.

Methodology & data

Research basis. The three core filters (term-structure slope, IV30/RV30, liquidity) come from the Volatility Vibes study of 72,500 earnings events (2007–present). Additions layered on top — execution-quality checks, sector-cohort conditioning, FOMC/regime gating, position sizing and a fee/slippage model — are documented on the How It Works tab.

Data sources. Earnings calendar from the Nasdaq public API (cross-checked against Yahoo per ticker); option chains, prices and implied vols from Yahoo Finance (~15-min delayed); VIX term structure and SPY for the market-regime read. There is no paid/historical option-chain feed, so a tick-level backtest isn't possible — the 10-year figures are a Monte Carlo calibrated to the published research moments, and the live paper tracker is the real out-of-sample test.

How it runs. A Python scanner regenerates the data roughly every 5 minutes during US market hours (via an external cron trigger) and commits it to GitHub; this page reads that JSON. Signals finalize near the close; trades are entered ~3:45 PM ET and exited ~9:45 AM ET the next session.

Honest limitations
  • Delayed mid-quotes grade the strategy, not real fills — actual executions differ, sometimes materially on illiquid names.
  • Illiquid back-month expiries can show unreliable debits; the scanner prefers liquid monthlies, but always verify at a broker.
  • The Monte Carlo inherits the published research's assumptions; treat differences between variants as the signal, not absolute dollar outcomes.
  • Short options carry early-assignment risk on the front leg (see How It Works for what it is and how it is managed).
  • Educational/research only — not investment advice. Options involve substantial risk of loss.
