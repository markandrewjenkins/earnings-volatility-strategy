The edge — selling earnings volatility

Quarterly earnings often account for the majority of a stock's annual volatility. Ahead of the event, hedgers (price-insensitive, mandated to buy protection) and speculators (buying short-dated calls) push option prices and implied volatility systematically above what the stock actually delivers. This strategy takes the other side: sell the inflated front-month IV just before the announcement, collect the IV crush after it.

Backed by research over 72,500 earnings events across 4,500 stocks (2007–present): traded blindly, both structures break even — the market prices earnings well on average. But three pre-event filters isolate a persistently profitable subset (~10–12% of all events):

1 · Term-structure slope

≤ −0.00406

Slope of ATM IV from the front expiry to the 45-day point. Steep backwardation = near-term options most overpriced relative to later ones. The single strongest predictor — it gates the whole trade.

2 · IV30 / RV30

≥ 1.25

30-day implied vol vs Yang-Zhang realized vol. If IV is already rich versus how the stock actually moves, it's even more likely to be overpriced for the event.

3 · Liquidity

≥ 1.5M shares

30-day average share volume. More participants → more price-insensitive hedging flow → richer premium. Higher-volume deciles showed better returns for both structures.

Execution timing
  • Entry: final ~15 minutes before the close of the session preceding the announcement (today's close for AMC reporters; also covers next-morning BMO reporters).
  • Exit: ~15 minutes after the next session's open — the "jump" exit. Holding until the next close was a consistent loser in the data (post-earnings announcement drift works against a short-vol position).
  • Strike: at-the-money at entry. Calendar gap: ~30 days — wide enough that back-month IV holds while front-month IV crushes.
Goal: raise win rate and per-trade return over the baseline three-filter model. The first three ideas are implemented live in this scanner as the TIER 1 ★ rating; the rest are candidates for the backtest queue.
Implemented in the scanner (Tier 1)

A · Premium richness vs history

Compare today's option-implied expected move against the stock's own average realized earnings move (last ~8 quarters). Require expected ≥ 1.15× historical. The baseline filters only prove IV is rich vs recent calm trading; this proves it's rich vs what earnings actually do to this stock — directly targeting per-trade return.

B · Execution-quality gate

Require ATM bid/ask spread ≤ 10% of mid. The research's biggest silent killer is slippage: a 7% mean edge disappears if you give up 4% crossing wide spreads twice. Directly raises realized (not theoretical) win rate.

C · Minimum share price $20

Cheap stocks have structurally wide relative spreads and coarse strike spacing, so the "ATM" calendar is often meaningfully off-strike. Filtering them removes a cluster of poor fills and off-model losses.

Backtest queue — most promising next

D · Rank, don't just gate

The decile study shows returns rise monotonically as slope gets more negative and IV/RV gets higher. When multiple candidates qualify on the same day, allocate to the top-ranked 1–3 by combined decile score instead of equally to all. Same capital, higher average edge per dollar.

E · Skip binary-catalyst names

Exclude sectors where the "earnings" event often carries FDA/regulatory-style tail risk (small biotech, crypto-levered names). These populate the catastrophic left tail of the straddle distribution — cutting them trims tail losses at minimal cost to frequency.

F · Earnings-day VIX regime filter

When index vol itself is spiking (VIX > ~28 or term structure inverted), single-stock back-month IV can crush along with the front, collapsing the calendar's edge. Standing aside (or switching to straddles, which don't need the back month to hold) in those regimes should smooth the equity curve.

G · Double calendar variant

For Tier 1 setups with expected move ≫ strike spacing, a double calendar (one at +EM strike, one at −EM) widens the profit zone and cuts gamma pain when the stock moves ~1× the expected move — trading a bit of peak return for win rate.

H · Scale by Kelly, cap correlation

Max 3 concurrent positions and ≤ ~18% of account in simultaneous earnings risk (3 × 6%). Multiple same-day trades are nearly independent bets — the research's variance numbers assume one at a time.

I · Exit discipline on the jump

Take the exit 15 min after the open mechanically. The data says the edge is fully realized in the jump; discretionary "let it ride" holding re-exposes you to post-earnings drift, which is a negative-edge period for short vol.
